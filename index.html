I am a Ph.D. candidate in Computational Neuroscience with a foundation in physics and mathematics. My research bridges theoretical modeling and real-world applications in brain function and neurological disorders. I focus on simulating brain network dynamics using spiking neuron models and investigating how structural properties influence information processing.

My ongoing projects have explored a variety of topics: how cortical timescales vary by brain region and layer, how short-range neural connections amplify long-range communication, and how inhibitory dynamics control network excitability. Through this work, I develop quantitative frameworks that contribute both to fundamental neuroscience and future brain-inspired computation systems.

Projects & Research

    This project investigates how timescales differ across cortical layers and regions, with sensory areas exhibiting faster dynamics and integrative areas showing slower processing. I built comparative spiking network models of the cortex in rodents, primates, and humans, helping uncover functional differences shaped by brain structure and evolution.
